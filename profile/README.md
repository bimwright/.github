# bimwright

Centuries ago, the suffix "-wright" carried a distinct and weighty meaning. It did not merely denote a laborer; it signified a master craftsman. The shipwright understood the invisible tensions of wood and water to build vessels that crossed oceans. The wheelwright forged iron and oak to carry the brutal weight of commerce across continents. A wright was a maker who possessed a deep, tactile relationship with their materials, shaping raw elements into structures that endured. They did not mass-produce; they carefully constructed the foundational tools and vehicles of their eras.

Today, the materials of construction have expanded beyond steel, timber, and concrete. The built environment is now constructed twice: first in data, then in physical reality. Building Information Modeling is our modern timber. Yet, the heavy digital machinery used to shape this data has grown rigid and isolated. Practitioners often find themselves trapped inside vendor walled gardens, forced to adapt their deep technical knowledge to the constraints of generic, bloated platforms. The true art of the discipline is obscured by black-box software designed for mass consumption rather than precision.

This is why bimwright exists. We reclaim the ethos of the artisan for the architecture, engineering, and construction disciplines. We are not a corporate vendor attempting to capture your data, nor are we building another generic cloud platform. We are craftsmen of code and logic.

By carefully machining open, precise Model Context Protocol servers, we build the vital bridges that allow heavy-duty AEC applications to communicate, reason, and operate with modern intelligence. We treat our tooling with the exactness of a physical craft. Every server we write is designed specifically for the practitioner — built to be reliable, transparent, and deliberately focused. We understand the complex, data-heavy engines of digital construction, and we hand-forge the parts that make them work for the builder, rather than the software vendor. We put control back into the hands of the architects, engineers, and modelers who actually design the world.

**Bimwright: Forging the digital craft of the built environment.**
